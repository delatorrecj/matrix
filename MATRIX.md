## 1. Executive Summary

Cities across ASEAN build infrastructure on instinct. Multi-billion-peso decisions about where to place a school, when to widen a road, whether to approve a tower, or how to route a flood-control project rely on static feasibility studies that age the day they are filed. The result is infrastructure that fails its community on the day it opens — congested terminals, displaced informal workers, lost green space, flooded barangays that the plan was supposed to protect.

**MATRIX is a pre-construction infrastructure impact simulator.** A planner, developer, or LGU officer drops a proposed project onto a simulator of their city. The system runs thousands of multi-agent simulations in under ninety seconds and returns scored, calibrated impact estimates across five dimensions — Behavioral, Social, Economic, Ecological, and Societal — each with explicit confidence intervals. The user sees the simulation play out visually on the map, reads dimension-specific impact narratives generated by Gemini 3.1, and exports a structured recommendation report.

Iloilo City is our pilot. It is the right ASEAN testbed for three reasons: it won the **2026 ASEAN Clean Tourist City Award** — its second time (after the 2020–2022 cycle), accepted on Jan 30, 2026 at the ASEAN Tourism Forum in Cebu — aligning our narrative with the AAIH 2026 theme *(PIA / Panay News, 2026 — see References)*; it is small enough (78 sq km, 180 barangays) to build a complete behavioral simulator in six weeks; and it sits at the center of a genuinely *suburban developing* metropolitan network — Metro Iloilo–Guimaras — where pre-construction simulation has the highest marginal value.

MATRIX scales across ASEAN through prompt-level adaptation: changing the pilot city from Iloilo to Jakarta, Bangkok, or Ho Chi Minh City requires swapping the OSM bounding box and reweighting the commuter persona archetypes — not rebuilding the platform.

## 2. The Problem MATRIX Solves

Infrastructure failure in ASEAN suburban cities is not a planning *skill* problem. It is a planning *visibility* problem. Three concrete failures define the current state:

**Static feasibility studies cannot anticipate emergent behavior.** A traffic count taken in 2022 will not predict how a new mall in 2026 reshapes the daily routine of every household within three kilometers. Spreadsheet models ignore the cascading second-order effects: which jeepney route gains demand, which barangay loses footfall, which informal vendor zone gets displaced, which flood corridor gets blocked.

**Cross-domain impacts are evaluated in silos.** Environmental Impact Assessments live in one office. Transport Impact Assessments live in another. Economic projections sit with the Treasury. Social equity reviews — if they happen at all — live with the LGU social welfare office. By the time these documents reach the same desk, the project is already approved. No tool currently lets a planner *simulate all five impact dimensions of the same project in one run*.

**Existing simulation tools require specialist expertise.** PTV Vissim and Aimsun need transport engineers. ESRI CityEngine needs GIS specialists. Replica needs data scientists. AnyLogic needs simulation modelers. None of these tools accept "what happens if we build a 3,000-seat school here?" as a natural-language input from a city planner with no technical background.

The cumulative cost of these failures is borne by the commuter who pays 25–35% of their daily fare for first-mile travel that the planners never modeled; by the informal vendor whose corner gets paved over because no one ran the displacement scenario; by the barangay that floods because the new development's runoff calculations were done in isolation; by the developer whose entrance opens onto the wrong side of the building because the pedestrian flow study used a sample size of three days. *(Montalbo, UP SURP; TRID 2013; JICA 2014/2019; ICLEI Iloilo Roadmap)*

## 4. The Five Impact Dimensions (Core Product)

Every MATRIX simulation produces calibrated impact scores across five dimensions. Each dimension carries an explicit confidence level (High / Medium / Low) based on data availability and methodological certainty. This is not false precision dressed as analytics — it is honest, dimension-aware impact intelligence.

### 4.1 Behavioral Impact (Confidence: typically **High**)
*How will people change their daily patterns?*
- Trip generation and redistribution across affected corridors
- Mode-share shifts (jeepney to PUJ, walk to tricycle, car to bike)
- Pedestrian flow changes at entrances and intersections
- Peak-hour saturation curves before and after
- Inputs: OSM network, partial GTFS, PSA demographics, persona-driven SUMO agents
- Output metric: Δ trips/day per corridor, with 95% confidence interval

### 4.2 Social Impact (Confidence: typically **Medium**)
*Who benefits, who is displaced, who gains or loses access?*
- Equity-weighted access scoring (proximity gains by income decile)
- Informal worker displacement risk (vendors, tricycle drivers, pedicab operators)
- Service accessibility (schools, hospitals, markets) by barangay
- Vulnerability index for elderly, PWD, low-income households
- Inputs: PSA APIS poverty data, barangay-level demographics, informal settler maps via FOI
- Output metric: Gini-style access inequality score, displacement risk count

### 4.3 Economic Impact (Confidence: typically **Medium**)
*How does the project ripple through the local economy?*
- Land value change projections within 1km radius
- Business footfall projections (gain/loss per affected zone)
- Employment effects: direct jobs created, indirect jobs displaced
- Tax base impact for the LGU (real property tax, business tax)
- Inputs: PSA income data, BIR municipal revenue, Colliers PH property reports, ADB/NEDA multipliers
- Output metric: Economic impact range in PHP (not a point estimate — a calibrated range)

### 4.4 Ecological Impact (Confidence: typically **High** for Iloilo)
*What happens to the environment and climate exposure?*
- Carbon emission delta (transport + building operations)
- Air quality impact at sensor stations (Iloilo has international-standard monitors)
- Green cover loss measured against Sentinel-2 baseline
- Flood risk redistribution (PAGASA/NOAH hazard layer overlay)
- Urban heat island contribution from new built surface
- Inputs: Sentinel-2 satellite imagery, PAGASA flood maps, NOAH hazards, WHO/EMEP emission factors
- Output metric: CO₂e tonnes/year, AQI delta at monitoring stations, hectares of green cover affected

### 4.5 Societal Impact (Confidence: typically **Low to Medium**)
*Quality of life, public health, heritage, cohesion.*
- Noise pollution exposure mapping (proximity proxies)
- Heritage zone proximity scoring (Calle Real, Molo Church, Jaro Cathedral, Iloilo River Esplanade)
- Public health proxy (PM2.5 exposure × population density)
- Walkability score change (Iloilo bike-capital framing)
- Cultural infrastructure impact
- Inputs: OSM heritage tags, air quality stations, population density rasters
- Output metric: Societal impact composite (0–100) with sub-scores per criterion

**Why explicit confidence levels matter.** Every academic mentor and every government planner has been burned by a tool that reports "the impact is 47.3 jobs lost" when the underlying data could not possibly support that precision. MATRIX reports "Economic impact: -PHP 8M to -PHP 14M over 5 years (Medium confidence; sensitive to assumption on tax-base multiplier — see model card)." This honesty is the academic-grade differentiator.

**C. Models, Frameworks, Tools**

- **Simulation kernel:** Eclipse SUMO via TraCI Python API
- **Persona generator:** Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite (free tier, high-volume calls)
- **Orchestrator and synthesis:** Gemini 3.1 Pro (NL parsing, report generation)
- **Knowledge graph:** ChromaDB + Microsoft GraphRAG (or LightRAG)
- **Predictive baseline:** XGBoost time-series for corridor volume forecasting
- **Bias auditor:** custom Python module — enforces mode-share anchor matching ground truth
- **Backend:** FastAPI + WebSocket for simulation streaming
- **Database:** Supabase Postgres (run metadata), ChromaDB (vectors), Redis (simulation cache)
- **Frontend:** Next.js 14 (App Router) + Tailwind + shadcn/ui
- **Map and animation:** Mapbox GL JS + Deck.gl TripsLayer for animated agent playback
- **Companion:** Progressive Web App (mobile-first GPS trace collection)

### 5.2 Real-Time Interactive Visualization (Option C — Committed)

The real-time interactive flow is the single highest-impact judging moment. We architect specifically for it:

**Latency budget: 90 seconds end-to-end.**
- 0–5 s: NL query parsing (Gemini 3.1 Pro)
- 5–15 s: Knowledge graph retrieval (GraphRAG)
- 15–60 s: Simulation execution (SUMO + persona pool)
- 60–80 s: Five impact modules compute in parallel
- 80–90 s: Synthesis agent generates narrative

**How we hit 90 seconds for real-time:**
1. **Persona pool pre-warmed at startup.** 200–500 commuter personas generated once and cached. User input triggers re-weighting, not regeneration.
2. **Delta simulations, not full reruns.** Baseline simulation runs nightly; user scenarios compute differential against baseline.
3. **Parallel module execution.** All five impact modules run concurrently on the same trajectory dataset.
4. **Streaming results.** Behavioral and Ecological return first (high confidence, fastest); Social, Economic, Societal stream in as they complete.
5. **Progressive UI.** Animation begins playing while later modules are still computing. User feels constant motion.

**Visualization layers (Deck.gl):**
- **Network layer** — base Iloilo road network
- **Project layer** — the proposed infrastructure highlighted
- **Trips layer** — animated agent trajectories during simulation playback
- **Impact heatmaps** — color-coded overlays per dimension (toggleable)
- **Confidence layer** — translucent fringe showing where data confidence is lower
- **Comparison slider** — drag to compare baseline vs scenario states

## 6. AI Approach and Model Justification

- **Primary AI techniques:** [x] Machine Learning, [x] Generative AI, [x] Agentic Simulation, [x] Retrieval-Augmented Generation
- **Models:**
  - Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite (high-volume persona generation, free tier)
  - Gemini 3.1 Pro (NL orchestration, synthesis, reasoning)
  - XGBoost (XGB baseline corridor volume forecaster)
  - Sentence Transformers `bge-small-en` (ChromaDB embeddings)
- **Frameworks:** Eclipse SUMO, OSMnx, Microsoft GraphRAG, ChromaDB

**Why this stack and not the alternatives:**
- **Why SUMO, not OASIS or MiroFish?** OASIS and MiroFish simulate social-media dynamics, not physical agents in cities. SUMO is the actual open-source urban mobility standard, developed by DLR (Germany), and supports intermodal simulation natively.
- **Why Gemini 3.1, not 2.0 or 1.5?** Gemini 1.5 is shut down, and Gemini 2.0 shut down June 1, 2026 — before our final submission. Gemini 3.1 Pro (released Feb 19, 2026) and 3.1 Flash-Lite are the current generation. Flash-Lite's free tier covers prototype scope.
- **Why one simulation kernel feeding five modules, not five separate simulators?** A unified trajectory dataset ensures the five dimensions are scored against the *same* simulated reality. Separate simulators would produce internal contradictions ("Behavioral says trips up, Ecological says emissions flat" because they ran different physics).

### 7.1 Data Sources (Iloilo-Specific, Open Where Possible)

- **Tier 2 (FOI-pending):** LTFRB Region VI route data, PSA APIS barangay-level, Iloilo City CLUP
- **Tier 3 (institutional outreach):** Clean Air Asia SMMR data inventory, ICLEI roadmap data
- **Tier 4 (academic baseline):** Calderon 2014 BRT model, Macalalag 2021 bike study, Philippine Geomatics 2021 population GIS

### 7.2 Data Quality and Confidence Anchoring

Every input dataset carries a confidence tier (High / Medium / Low) propagated through to the impact module outputs. The confidence layer in the UI surfaces this — users see *where* the simulation is sure and where it is estimating.

### 7.3 Licensing and Legality

- OSM under ODbL with attribution
- PSA data under open government data policy
- Sentinel-2 under ESA Copernicus license (free)
- Gemini API under Google AUP
- All open libraries under MIT, Apache 2.0, or EPL 2.0
- FOI-obtained data per Executive Order No. 2 (s. 2016)
- Volunteer PWA GPS traces collected with explicit opt-in, anonymized at device level
- Compliance with Philippine Data Privacy Act (RA 10173)

### 7.4 Bias Mitigation and Fairness

This is treated as a first-class feature, not a footnote.

**Identified risks:**
- LLM persona generation over-represents middle-class private-vehicle commuters (training-data bias)
- Formal transit over-documented relative to informal (jeepney, tricycle, pedicab)
- Economic impact modules biased toward measurable outputs (formal jobs) vs informal economy
- English-language prompts under-represent vernacular (Hiligaynon, Kinaray-a) commuter behavior
- Sentinel-2 cloud cover gaps systematically affect tropical city ecological scoring

**Mitigations:**
- **Mode-share anchor** — Persona generation constrained to match Iloilo's ground-truth mode share. Deviations beyond ±3% trigger reweighting.
- **Bias auditor module** — Runs after every persona batch. Public audit log.
- **Bilingual prompting** — Personas generated in English, Filipino, and Hiligaynon prompts to surface vernacular decision logic.
- **Informal economy module** — Explicit inclusion of informal vendor displacement and tricycle/pedicab operator impact in Social and Economic dimensions.
- **Confidence floor** — Any dimension scoring below confidence threshold is flagged "directional only" in the UI, not reported as precise.
- **Open methodology** — Every simulation logs its assumptions, data sources, and confidence anchors. Reproducible.

## 10. Scalability and Regional Resilience

**Geographic scaling is API-level.** New city = new OSM bounding box + new municipal data source. Simulation engine unchanged.

**Behavioral scaling is prompt-level.** Persona generator swaps from "Iloilo jeepney + modern PUJ + bike commuter" to "Jakarta ojek + angkot commuter" through a prompt swap. Mode-share anchors update with city-specific ground-truth percentages.

**Dimensional scaling is module-level.** New impact dimensions (e.g., Cultural Heritage, Energy, Water) plug into the same simulation kernel without disrupting existing modules.

**No physical infrastructure.** No IoT sensors, no on-site hardware. Pure cloud + open data + LLM. ASEAN deployment cost is API tokens, not hardware procurement. Because it is a simulator and not a real-time digital twin, it does not require constant live telemetry feeds.

**ASEAN target cities post-Iloilo:**

| City | Distinctive infrastructure pattern | Open data status |
|---|---|---|
| Jakarta | Ojek, angkot, bajaj, BRT TransJakarta | TransJakarta GTFS, OSM strong |
| Bangkok | Songthaew, tuk-tuk, motorcycle taxi, BTS/MRT | Strong GTFS, OSM strong |
| Ho Chi Minh City | Xe ôm, xe buýt, metro under construction | Limited GTFS, OSM strong |
| Kuala Lumpur | Rapid KL, bas mini, e-hailing | Rapid KL GTFS, OSM strong |

**Honest technical constraints:**

- **Cost of agentic simulation at scale.** Thousands of LLM personas per scenario hits real Gemini API cost. Mitigation: Flash-Lite for high-volume persona work; Pro only for orchestration. Persona pool cached, not regenerated.
- **Real-time latency under load.** 90-second budget holds for single user; multi-user load requires queueing. Acceptable for hackathon scope.
- **Confidence floor for low-data dimensions.** Societal scoring in cities with sparse heritage data will return "Low confidence — directional only." This is honest, not weak.
- **FOI lag.** LTFRB Region VI data may arrive after final submission. Hackathon build uses what's open; production deployment fills gaps over months.
